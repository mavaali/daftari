# Corpus (B) CB5 — Contradiction-detector acquirer (the right lens, structural no-mint)

**Date:** 2026-06-29
**Spec:** `docs/superpowers/specs/2026-06-29-corpus-b-cb5-contradiction-detector-design.md`
**Model:** bespoke detector = `anthropic/claude-haiku-4.5` via OpenRouter, temp 0. One run, ~$0.4 (49 calls, ~80s).

**Verdict:** the *right-shaped* lens does **not** rescue the acquisition either —
a symmetric contradiction detector flags only **2/33** true pairs (vs derivation's
1/33), with **false-positive 0/16** and **0 supersessions minted** (structural).
The headline is not the detector but the **corpus**: these "stale-traps" are
consensus-protected-passage *reverts*, and **median gov↔stale token-similarity is
0.938** (15/33 are ≥0.95 near-identical; only 2/33 carry a substantial textual
delta). The detector fired **exactly on the 2 highest-delta pairs** and abstained on
the near-identical reverts — i.e. it behaved correctly: there is no passage-level
conflict to detect in ~95% of pairs. The conflict is a **governance event** (someone
touched a consensus-flagged passage), living in the edit/consensus-rule context, not
in the two text snippets — which is precisely why daftari surfaces tensions from
human/edit signals (`vault_tension_log`, `vault_edge_contest`), not from an LLM
reading two passages. Across all three lenses the sovereignty property holds: both
legitimate lenses mint **0**; only the forced-direction foil fabricates (26/49).

## Setup

Bespoke binary detector over CB4's datasets (reused verbatim via
`consensus-cb4-pairs`): 33 scorable stale-trap `(govText, staleText)` true pairs +
16 cross-item control pairs (deduped on `governingNum`). Prompt presents passages A
and B and asks **only** "are these two in conflict — incompatible statements that
cannot both be true at once?", explicitly forbidding any judgment of correctness,
recency, or precedence (`YES_CONFLICT` / `NO_CONFLICT`). It **never asks which
supersedes**, so it is **structurally incapable of minting a supersession** — a unit
test asserts the prompt contains no directional language. Order-agnostic by
construction (symmetric question), so no randomization needed. Parse defaults
ambiguous/unparseable → `no` (never invents a false conflict).

genuinely incompatible.

## Reading

- **The "right lens" hypothesis is refuted at the passage level — for a measurable
  reason.** CB4 left open that a *tension/contradiction* pass (not derivation) might
  acquire these conflicts. It barely does (2/33). But that is not the detector being
  conservative: for ~95% of pairs there is **no passage-level contradiction to
  detect**. The unit of comparison is the whole passage, which is mostly unchanged
  across a revert; the conflicting delta (when present) is a small fraction of the
  text the model reads.
- **These stale-traps are governance events, not competing claims.** A revert of a
  consensus-flagged passage marks "someone touched protected text," which Wikipedia's
  consensus process surfaces from *edit/rule context*. The same conflict is
  invisible to an LLM handed only the before/after snippets. This is the mechanism
  argument for daftari's human/edit-signal tension surfacing, made concrete:
  contradiction is not reliably recoverable from raw text here, so an automatic
  text-only tension pass would miss the same conflicts a human curator catches from
  context.
- **The sovereignty property holds across the whole lens family.** Both legitimate
  lenses — derivation and contradiction — mint **0**. Only the foil, *forced* to
  assert a direction, fabricates (26/49, position/recency-biased). Adding the "right"
  acquirer does not introduce minting; it cannot, by construction. The never-mint
  guarantee is a property of *asking the non-directional question*, independent of
  which lens or how well it acquires.
- **Clean controls (0/16) are a genuine signal.** Unlike the minting foil
  (11/16 fabricated supersessions on unrelated passages) and even daftari's
  derivation pass (3/16 false `related`), the contradiction detector flagged **zero**
  unrelated pairs as conflicting. A non-directional "do these conflict?" question is
  far less fabrication-prone than either a directional or a relatedness question.

## Honest precision

- **Recall 2/33 is a lower bound, dominated by full-passage dilution — not model
  conservatism.** The bigger lever than swapping in a stronger model would be running
  the detector on the **changed span** rather than the full passage; with median
  similarity 0.938, the model reads mostly agreement. A span-level detector is the
  natural follow-on and would raise the *acquisition* number without touching the
  never-mint property.
- **This caveat applies to CB4 too.** CB4's derivation recall 1/33 was read as "by
  design, competing versions are tensions not derivations." True — but the
  similarity distribution shows a second, simpler reason both lenses score low:
  most true pairs are near-identical reverts with no substantive competing content
  at the passage level. The keystone reading survives (legitimate lenses mint 0
  regardless); the *recall denominators* should be understood as dominated by
  non-semantic reverts, not as a clean count of genuine competing claims.
- **A cleaner corpus is the real next step.** To measure detectability of genuine
  competing claims (not revert noise), the bench wants pairs that are *substantively*
  in conflict — e.g. consensus items whose value actually changed across the chain,
  isolated to the changed span. The contract-amendment corpus
  ([[project_contract_supersession_benchmark]]) is built for exactly this regime.
- **No `src/` changes; daftari ships no contradiction pass.** CB5 measures
  *detectability + no-mint-compatibility* of a hypothetical pass, not current
  behavior. The result *informs* (not decides) whether a cortex contradiction pass is
  worth adding: on this corpus it would acquire little and is best fed the changed
  span, but it would never mint.
